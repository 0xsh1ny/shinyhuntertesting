Q4 Give an Introduction of Bhagavad Gita in detail. 15 marks ▾

Introduction / Background

The Bhagavad Gita is one of the most sacred texts of Hindu philosophy. It is part of the Mahabharata, specifically in the Bhishma Parva (chapters 23–40). It contains 18 chapters and 700 verses (shlokas). The word "Gita" means song, and "Bhagavad" means of God — so it is the "Song of God."

The Setting

The Gita takes place on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, just before the great war between the Pandavas and Kauravas. Arjuna sees his relatives, teachers, and loved ones on the opposite side. He is overcome with grief and confusion (Vishada) and refuses to fight. He puts down his bow Gandiva and surrenders to Krishna for guidance.

Teacher and Student

  • Teacher: Lord Sri Krishna (charioteer and guide)
  • Student: Arjuna (warrior, devotee)

This dialogue is also called Krishnarjuna Samvad.

18 Chapters — Key Ones

ChapterNameTheme
Ch. 1Arjuna Vishada YogaArjuna's grief and confusion
Ch. 2Sankhya YogaSelf-knowledge, soul is eternal
Ch. 3Karma YogaPath of selfless action
Ch. 9Raja Vidya Raja Guhya YogaRoyal knowledge, devotion

Ch. 18Moksha Sanyasa YogaLiberation, conclusion

Core Message — Three Paths

  • Gyana Yoga — path of knowledge and wisdom
  • Karma Yoga — path of action without attachment
  • Bhakti Yoga — path of devotion to God
Central teaching: "Do your duty without attachment to results" — Nishkama Karma

Relevance and Legacy

The Gita is not just a religious text — it is a guide for life. Leaders like Mahatma Gandhi, Swami Vivekananda, and Bal Gangadhar Tilak drew deep inspiration from it. It teaches how to handle duty, stress, relationships, and purpose with equanimity.

Q7 Write the relevance of Bhagavad Gita in the current era. 15 marks ▾

Introduction

The Bhagavad Gita was spoken thousands of years ago, but its message is timeless. In today's fast-paced, stressful modern world, the Gita is more relevant than ever.

1. Stress and Mental Health

Modern life is full of anxiety, depression, and confusion. The Gita teaches Sthitaprajna — a person of stable mind who is not shaken by sorrow or excited by happiness. This is exactly what today's mental health framework calls emotional resilience.

2. Karma Yoga in Professional Life

"Do your work sincerely, do not worry about results."

This directly applies to students and professionals who are obsessed with outcomes and suffer when results don't meet expectations. The Gita teaches effort over obsession.

3. Leadership and Decision Making

Arjuna's dilemma is the dilemma of every leader — choosing between personal emotion and larger duty. Krishna's guidance teaches decisive, value-based leadership. Many corporate training programs today use Gita principles.

4. Concept of Swadharma

Everyone has their own role and duty in society. The Gita says fulfilling your own duty (Swadharma) honestly is better than imitating someone else's path — highly relevant in today's identity crisis among youth.

5. Equality and Non-discrimination

Krishna says the soul (Atma) has no caste, religion, or gender. This teaches universal equality and brotherhood — values urgently needed in today's divided world.

6. Environmental Awareness

The Gita teaches balance with nature and warns against greed — relevant to today's climate crisis and overconsumption culture.

Conclusion

The Gita is not a book of the past. It is a manual for life. Whether managing emotions, performing duties, or finding purpose — the Gita provides answers. Its relevance only grows with time.

Q2 Describe the nature of Yoga in Bhagavad Gita + Discuss Trigunas. 10 + 5 = 15 marks ▾

Part A — Nature of Yoga (10 marks)

The word Yoga comes from Sanskrit root "Yuj" meaning to unite. In the Gita, Yoga means union of the individual soul (Jivatma) with the Supreme Soul (Paramatma).

Krishna defines Yoga as:

"Yogah Karmasu Kaushalam" — Yoga is excellence in action

"Samatvam Yoga Uchyate" — Yoga is equanimity of mind

Three Main Yogas

  • Gyana Yoga (Path of Knowledge): Attaining liberation through self-knowledge. Understanding the difference between body and soul, real and unreal, eternal and temporary.
  • Karma Yoga (Path of Action): Performing all actions as duty, without selfish desire or attachment to results. Offering all actions to God. Most practical yoga for ordinary people.
  • Bhakti Yoga (Path of Devotion): Surrendering completely to God with love and faith. Krishna calls this the highest and easiest path. Pure devotion melts all ego and leads to liberation.

Other Yogas Mentioned

  • Dhyana Yoga — meditation and control of mind
  • Raja Yoga — royal path combining all forms
Key principle: Detachment from results + Dedication of action to God = Liberation (Moksha)

Part B — Trigunas (5 marks)

According to Gita Chapter 14 (Gunatraya Vibhaga Yoga), all of nature and human personality is made of three qualities called Trigunas:

GunaNatureEffect on Person
TamasDarkness, laziness, ignoranceDelusion, sleep, inaction
RajasPassion, desire, activityAttachment, restlessness, greed
SattvaPurity, clarity, goodnessKnowledge, peace, happiness

Every person has all three gunas, but one dominates. The goal of spiritual life is to rise above all three gunas (Trigunatita) and reach the pure state of the soul.

Q3 Describe the tradition of Karmayoga + Nature of Yagya in Bhagavad Gita. 5 + 10 = 15 marks ▾

Part A — Tradition of Karmayoga (5 marks)

Karma Yoga is the central teaching of the Gita. Krishna tells Arjuna in Chapter 3:

"You have the right to perform your duty, but not to the fruits of your actions." — Nishkama Karma

Key Principles of Karma Yoga

  • Do your duty as per your Swadharma (own role/duty)
  • Do not be attached to success or failure
  • Dedicate all actions to God
  • Work itself becomes worship
  • Act for the welfare of all — Lokasangraha

Krishna also says inaction is not an option — even maintaining your body requires action. The wise person acts without ego.

Part B — Nature of Yagya / Sacrifice (10 marks)

In the Gita, Yagya doesn't only mean fire ritual. Krishna expands its meaning to cover all selfless acts performed for a higher purpose.

Types of Yagya

#TypeMeaning
1Dravya YagyaSacrifice of material things / wealth
2Gyana YagyaSacrifice through knowledge and wisdom
3Dhyana YagyaSacrifice through meditation
4Tapas YagyaSacrifice through self-discipline / austerity
5Pranayama YagyaOffering the breath / life force as sacrifice
Core idea: Any action done selflessly for a higher purpose, without ego, is a Yagya. Life itself should be lived as a Yagya — a continuous offering.

Krishna declares that Gyana Yagya is the highest of all — because all material sacrifice ultimately leads to knowledge, and knowledge leads to liberation.

Bonus — Q3(a) Sthitaprajna · 5 marks

Q3a Describe in detail upon the topic Sthitaprajna. 5 marks ▾

Sthitaprajna (स्थितप्रज्ञ) means "one of steady wisdom" — a person whose mind is stable and unshaken in all circumstances. Described in Chapter 2 of the Gita.

Qualities of a Sthitaprajna

  • Not troubled by sorrow, not excited by happiness
  • Free from attachment, fear, and anger
  • Without selfish desires — like a lamp in a windless place, steady and still
  • Withdraws senses from sense objects like a tortoise withdraws its limbs
  • Acts in the world but is not affected by it — neither elated by good nor depressed by bad
A Sthitaprajna is the ideal human being according to the Gita — fully aware, fully active, yet completely at peace.

This concept is the Gita's answer to the modern problem of emotional instability, anxiety, and reactivity.
